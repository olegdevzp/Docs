## Part One: The Rediscovery of Trauma

### Chapter 1: Lessons from Vietnam Veterans

#### Chapter 1 summary

Bessel van der Kolk opens his account of trauma’s rediscovery by placing the reader in the late 1970s, when psychiatry was still poorly equipped to explain why some people remained tormented long after danger had passed. He describes arriving in Boston and beginning work that would bring him face to face with a generation of men who had been sent to war and returned with bodies and minds that would not stand down. The chapter’s title signals that the lessons would be learned not from textbooks first, but from veterans whose suffering was concrete, repetitive, and stubbornly resistant to reassurance. Vietnam veterans became an unexpected laboratory for understanding post-traumatic stress because their injuries were not only political and moral but also unmistakably physiological. Many clinicians had treated anxiety and depression before, yet the constellation they saw in these men did not fit neatly into the familiar categories of the era.

Patients could look rational in an interview and still be seized by sudden rage, panic, or a blank shutdown that seemed to come from nowhere. Some could describe their past with clarity and still feel as if the past were happening again in their muscles, breath, and startle reflex. The chapter argues that this mismatch between story and symptom is one of trauma’s defining signatures. Van der Kolk emphasizes that trauma is not simply a bad memory that can be argued away with insight. He suggests that trauma installs itself in systems that evolved for survival, not for narrative coherence.

The survival systems can keep scanning for threat even when the mind knows the war is over. That persistence turns everyday life into a terrain of triggers that civilians and even therapists may not recognize. The veterans’ experiences therefore forced a confrontation with the limits of talk therapy as the sole technology of cure. If words alone could not reliably reset a body stuck on high alert, then the field needed new models. The chapter frames this as a scientific and humanitarian problem at once.

It is scientific because the symptoms invited measurement, theory, and research. It is humanitarian because misrecognition often looked like blame. Men were labeled unmotivated, antisocial, or character disordered when their behavior was driven by invisible alarms. The clinical encounter thus became a place where society’s judgments about strength and weakness were enacted on individual bodies. Van der Kolk portrays the clinic as a front line where political history entered the consulting room in the form of nightmares, flashbacks, and broken relationships.

He also portrays clinicians as initially uncertain, because official diagnostic language lagged behind lived experience. Before post-traumatic stress disorder existed as a named diagnosis, people suffered without a shared vocabulary that could legitimate their pain. The absence of a name increased isolation and shame. The chapter sets out to show how naming, measuring, and understanding trauma began with listening to those who carried it. The Vietnam War was not a single event but a prolonged exposure to violence, loss, unpredictability, and moral conflict.

Many service members faced situations where survival and conscience pulled in different directions. Some returned to a country that did not welcome them uniformly, compounding injury with social rejection. The chapter suggests that trauma is not only what happened in combat but also what happened afterward in families, jobs, and communities. A veteran might be hypervigilant in a supermarket aisle because vigilance had once been adaptive. The same vigilance might be misread as paranoia or hostility by people who did not share the veteran’s learning history.

Sleep disruption became a nightly referendum on safety, with nightmares replaying scenes the waking mind tried to avoid. Startle responses could be extreme because the nervous system had been trained to treat sudden sound as potential threat. Concentration could collapse under the weight of intrusive imagery and bodily arousal. Relationships suffered when intimacy felt unsafe or when anger erupted with little warning. Substance use often entered the picture as self-medication against intolerable inner activation.

The chapter treats addiction not only as a moral failing but as an attempted solution to dysregulation. Isolation could deepen when veterans anticipated misunderstanding or disgust from others. Some veterans described feeling permanently different, as if they carried a secret knowledge about human violence. Guilt and survivor’s guilt threaded through many stories, complicating recovery with questions of worthiness. The chapter shows how trauma can distort the sense of time, collapsing past and present.

A flashback is not merely remembering; it can be re-embodied conviction that danger is now. Clinicians learned to distinguish ordinary anxiety from a state in which the body rehearses survival responses. They also learned that calm explanations rarely reached the parts of the brain driving those responses. This observation would later motivate body-based and sensory approaches elsewhere in the book. Yet in the early work with veterans, the first step was simply to take the body seriously as a source of information.

The chapter insists that ignoring somatic symptoms meant missing the core of the problem. It also insists that compassion must extend to behaviors that look irrational until their survival logic is understood. The legacy of Vietnam thus becomes a lesson in humility for any society that sends people into harm’s way. The chapter traces the slow emergence of post-traumatic stress disorder as a formal diagnosis. Before PTSD, suffering after trauma was often fragmented across labels that failed to capture the whole syndrome.

Some veterans received diagnoses that emphasized personality rather than injury. Others were caught in bureaucratic battles about service connection and compensation. Van der Kolk describes how clinical observation and advocacy pushed the profession toward a unified construct. The inclusion of PTSD in the DSM-III in 1980 was a milestone, not an ending. A diagnosis can validate, yet it can also narrow if people mistake the label for the full reality of lived experience.

The chapter suggests that categories help research and treatment planning but never replace individual stories. Once PTSD existed as a named entity, researchers could begin comparing groups, testing treatments, and refining theory. The veterans’ cohort also made trauma visible in a culture that preferred not to think about war’s psychological costs. Political dimensions were unavoidable because acknowledging widespread post-war suffering had implications for policy and accountability. The chapter implies that what gets counted as injury is never purely medical.

Funding, stigma, and national mythologies shape who receives care and who is suspected of exaggeration. Women and other trauma survivors would later benefit from frameworks forged in part through veteran advocacy. The chapter therefore positions Vietnam work as part of a larger movement toward trauma-informed understanding. Still, early PTSD constructs centered heavily on fear-based symptoms familiar from combat contexts. Clinicians would eventually broaden the map to include developmental trauma and complex presentations.

Van der Kolk uses the opening chapter to foreshadow that expansion without losing the clarity of the original lesson. He stresses that trauma can change basic trust in the world. It can alter the felt sense of safety in ordinary environments like crowds, bridges, or confined spaces. It can also alter identity, leaving people unsure who they are outside the roles of warrior or survivor. The chapter repeatedly returns to the idea that trauma is stored in the body’s habits of protection.

Those habits include bracing, scanning, shutting down, and explosive mobilization. The therapeutic task implied here is not only to tell the story but to help the body relearn safety. The chapter’s opening lessons thus anticipate the book’s later emphasis on integration across brain, mind, and body. Van der Kolk describes an era when psychiatry was becoming more biological yet still often split mind from body in practice. The veterans pushed clinicians toward integrative thinking because symptoms arrived as palpable facts.

Sweating, trembling, and heart pounding were not metaphors. They were measurements written in flesh. The chapter suggests that paying attention to autonomic arousal became a gateway to understanding trauma’s persistence. Researchers began to ask why some people recovered after extreme events while others remained altered for years. The question of vulnerability intersected with genetics, prior adversity, social support, and chance.

The chapter avoids reducing trauma to a single cause, emphasizing interaction between person and context. It also avoids romanticizing resilience as purely individual virtue. Social conditions that offer safety and belonging make recovery more plausible. When those conditions are absent, symptoms can harden into chronic patterns. Early stress research is woven into the narrative as background for later neuroscience chapters.

Even before advanced imaging, clinicians could see that trauma changed attention. Traumatized people might miss neutral cues while amplifying threat cues. They might also dissociate under overload, appearing absent or numb when overwhelmed. The chapter treats dissociation as a protective strategy with long-term costs. It treats hyperarousal as a protective strategy that can exhaust relationships and health.

Therapists learned that insight alone rarely stopped a nightmare. Patients could agree intellectually that they were safe and still wake in terror. That gap between cognition and bodily state became a central puzzle. The chapter proposes that trauma disrupts the coordination of brain systems responsible for evaluating danger. It proposes that time stamping and contextualization can fail, leaving memories that feel present.

The therapeutic implication is that treatment must address the neurobiology of fear, not only the narrative of what happened. The chapter closes its scientific opening argument by insisting that the body keeps its own score in stress chemistry, muscle tension, and vigilance. The chapter conveys the emotional texture of clinical work with traumatized veterans. Trust could be slow because institutions had often failed them before. A therapist’s calm voice could help, yet it could also feel insufficient when inner alarms were screaming.

Some veterans tested boundaries because predictability felt safer than kindness that might vanish. Others withdrew because closeness felt dangerous or undeserved. The chapter shows how trauma can distort the ability to receive care. It also shows how repeated small experiences of safety can matter. Van der Kolk emphasizes listening without rushing to interpret.

He emphasizes bearing witness without forcing forgiveness or premature closure. The chapter acknowledges anger toward systems that sent young people into moral and physical peril. It acknowledges grief for lost friends and lost innocence. It acknowledges shame that does not respond neatly to reassurance. Therapists had to learn toleration for affect intensity without becoming punitive or frightened.

They had to learn that stabilization sometimes precedes exploration. The chapter foreshadows later discussions of stages and sequencing in trauma treatment. It also foreshadows controversies about memory and exposure. Even in chapter one, the reader senses that healing will require more than a single technique. The chapter offers hope by showing that understanding itself can reduce isolation.

Naming an experience as trauma can reconnect a person to humanity rather than defectiveness. Legitimizing suffering can reduce the secondary pain of being disbelieved. The chapter suggests that society-level recognition parallels individual validation. When trauma is denied collectively, survivors are forced to carry it alone. When it is acknowledged, room appears for repair and reform.

The lessons from Vietnam veterans thus become lessons for any setting where trauma hides in plain sight. The chapter invites readers to compare combat trauma with other forms of overwhelming adversity. Many mechanisms overlap even when contexts differ. A child’s nervous system can learn vigilance from neglect as a soldier learns it from ambush. The book will later explore developmental timing and attachment, but chapter one establishes a core theme.

The theme is that overwhelming threat reshapes the biology of experience. It reshapes what feels safe, what feels possible, and what feels meaningful. Van der Kolk uses veterans’ stories to argue against shame-based explanations of post-traumatic symptoms. He argues against treating survival responses as mere personality flaws. He also argues against therapies that implicitly blame patients for not getting better quickly.

The chapter frames trauma as an injury that can be studied, not a weakness that must be hidden. It frames recovery as a process that may be slow and nonlinear. Setbacks become understandable when trauma is understood as a learned bodily state. Relapses become understandable when triggers are subtle and pervasive. The chapter encourages curiosity about what a patient’s behavior is trying to prevent.

It encourages clinicians to ask what would make the body feel safer, not only what story would make sense. That shift from interpretation alone to regulation and safety is a thread running through the entire book. Chapter one also warns against sensationalism. Suffering is not a spectacle; it is a call for skillful care. The author’s tone mixes moral urgency with disciplined observation.

He wants science to serve compassion rather than replace it. He wants clinicians to remain humble about what they do not yet know. He wants society to stop abandoning people after inflicting or ignoring their wounds. The chapter’s cumulative point is that listening to veterans changed psychiatry’s imagination of trauma. It changed what clinicians measured, what they feared, and what they hoped treatment could do.

By the end of the opening chapter, the reader has a working picture of trauma as more than a memory problem. Trauma is portrayed as a condition of the entire organism. It affects emotions, identity, relationships, and physiology together. The veterans taught that war continues in the nervous system long after the last shot. They taught that peace requires more than a treaty signed abroad.

Peace can require rebuilding trust at the level of sleep, appetite, and touch. The chapter prepares the reader for later explanations of brain networks involved in fear and self-awareness. It prepares the reader for trauma’s impact on children and on memory. It prepares the reader for therapeutic approaches that go beyond talking. Yet none of those later developments erase the primacy of the first lesson.

The first lesson is that trauma must be met with seriousness at the level of lived bodily experience. Van der Kolk returns repeatedly to the ethical dimension of recognition. Recognition does not cure by itself, but misrecognition can harm. Diagnostic labels can help or hurt depending on how they are used in a person’s life. The chapter suggests that the best use of diagnosis is to open doors to care and research.

The worst use is to reduce a person to a label while ignoring context. The veterans’ stories also highlight camaraderie and loss. They highlight the moral weight of survival when others did not survive. They highlight the difficulty of returning to civilian rhythms after training for extremes. The chapter’s emotional arc moves from confusion toward clarity about what kind of problem trauma is.

It moves from isolation toward the possibility of shared understanding. It moves from blame toward a more accurate science of stress and recovery. The reader finishes chapter one with a question that propels the rest of the book. The question is how modern treatment can help traumatized people feel fully alive in the present again. The answer, suggested only in outline here, will require brain science, relational healing, and body-wise practice woven together.

The chapter’s historical placement matters because psychiatry’s tools in the 1970s were uneven for trauma-related suffering. Medications sometimes dampened symptoms without addressing their meaning or context. Dynamic therapies sometimes explored childhood without connecting to the adult’s current physiological state. Behavioral approaches sometimes focused on symptoms without honoring the depth of moral pain. The veterans complicated every simplistic model because they brought real-world complexity into the office.

They brought combat’s noise, dirt, blood, and randomness into stories that could not be tidied. They also brought pride, duty, loyalty, and love among fellow service members. Trauma narratives are never only narratives of damage; they are narratives of what people endured for the sake of belonging. Van der Kolk’s early lessons include respect for the veteran’s world as it was lived. That respect becomes a template for respectful trauma work in other populations.

The chapter hints that shame often attaches to what people did to survive. It hints that moral injury can be as haunting as fear. It hints that forgiveness may be less urgent than safety and integration. The body’s scorekeeping includes guilt’s physical weight as well as fear’s adrenaline. Therapists learned to pace their questions because inquiry itself could flood a patient.

They learned that stability skills could be lifesaving before any deep excavation of memory. They learned that trust is built through reliability, not through eloquence alone. The chapter therefore embeds a pragmatic ethic inside its scientific story. It suggests that good treatment matches the patient’s capacity in the moment. It suggests that pushing too fast can recreate the violence of overwhelm.

It suggests that slowness can be a form of respect. The reader sees trauma care as craft, not only as theory. The chapter also warns against cynicism. Even imperfect therapies and imperfect systems can still offer meaningful help. Hope is not naive optimism; it is commitment to continued learning.

The veterans’ lessons include that recovery is possible for many, though not guaranteed for all. Suffering can be reduced even when it cannot be erased. Dignity can be restored even when losses remain permanent. The chapter’s final emphasis lands on integration. Integration means living with memory without being possessed by it.

### Chapter 2: Revolutions in Understanding Mind and Brain

#### Chapter 2 summary

Chapter two widens the lens from Vietnam veterans to the long intellectual history behind modern trauma science. It asks how Western medicine and psychiatry came to picture suffering as a problem of mind, brain, or both. Van der Kolk traces revolutions in understanding that made trauma thinkable in new ways across centuries. The chapter’s title promises upheaval, not smooth progress, because ideas about mental life have often collided. Readers encounter the tension between viewing people as rational agents and viewing them as organisms shaped by forces they do not fully control.

That tension still shapes debates about responsibility, blame, and care. The chapter connects historical psychiatry to contemporary neuroscience without pretending the past was merely primitive. Earlier clinicians often observed carefully, even when their theories sound strange to modern ears. Their categories reflected the moral and scientific assumptions of their times. Understanding those assumptions clarifies why trauma was sometimes seen, sometimes denied, and often misnamed.

The chapter surveys shifting ideas about hysteria and trauma-related symptoms in women and men. It shows how gendered assumptions distorted recognition of pain that did not fit expected scripts. It also shows how war and disaster periodically forced societies to reconsider what minds could endure. World War I’s shell shock is one turning point where bodily and mental breakdown could no longer be ignored en masse. The sheer scale of impairment challenged purely moral explanations of cowardice.

Yet even then, suspicion and stigma followed many sufferers. The chapter implies that social readiness to believe shapes what counts as a legitimate diagnosis. Scientific evidence alone does not float free from culture. Van der Kolk weaves biography and history together to show how institutions and politics steer clinical imagination. He describes figures who tried to map traumatic memory, dissociation, and bodily expression with the tools available to them.

Some pioneers emphasized observation of signs and symptoms in the consulting room. Others emphasized theory first and bent cases to fit. The chapter invites skepticism toward any single master narrative of mental life. It also invites respect for incremental correction over time. The rise of psychoanalysis enters the story as both an opening and a narrowing.

Freud’s early work engaged trauma-like phenomena and the body’s expression of distress. Later emphases within psychoanalytic traditions sometimes drifted from traumatic events toward fantasy and internal conflict. The chapter does not dismiss psychoanalysis wholesale. It situates it historically and asks what was gained and what was lost for trauma survivors. Janet’s contributions appear as an important alternative thread emphasizing dissociation and fragmentation of self.

Janet’s language sometimes fits trauma presentations more directly than models centered on repression alone. The chapter suggests that intellectual fashion can bury useful distinctions for decades. When dissociation returned to prominence in late twentieth-century trauma work, older insights were rediscovered rather than invented from scratch. The mind–brain relationship is a recurring puzzle throughout the chapter. Sometimes “mind” names subjective experience and meaning.

Sometimes “brain” names material mechanisms and constraints. The chapter argues against treating these as enemies. It argues that trauma demands bridges between levels of description. A purely brain-only vocabulary risks sounding like people are machines without inner life. A purely mind-only vocabulary risks ignoring how stress hormones and neural circuits constrain choices.

Van der Kolk uses the chapter to prepare readers for later neuroscience chapters without pretending biology explains everything. He emphasizes that trauma can alter information processing in ways that feel like moral failure from the outside. Hypervigilance can look like irritability. Numbing can look like coldness. Dissociation can look like laziness or manipulation when it is a survival maneuver.

The chapter’s historical survey helps explain why trauma survivors were often judged rather than helped. Psychiatry’s categories have sometimes punished difference by labeling it disease. They have sometimes denied disease by labeling suffering as personality. The chapter shows how diagnostic boundaries moved as wars, feminism, advocacy, and research pressed institutions. The DSM’s evolution appears not as pure enlightenment but as contested revision.

Each edition reflects compromises among clinicians, researchers, insurers, and social anxieties. Van der Kolk highlights moments when trauma-related presentations were split across multiple diagnoses. That splitting could fragment care and obscure common underlying processes. The chapter encourages thinking about transdiagnostic patterns beneath surface labels. It encourages clinicians to listen for trauma histories even when patients arrive with depression, anxiety, or somatic complaints.

The history of “conversion” and medically unexplained symptoms appears as part of trauma’s shadow record. Patients with bodily symptoms without clear organic findings were often caught between specialties. They could be dismissed by physicians and pathologized by psychiatrists. The chapter portrays that limbo as itself traumatizing. It suggests that modern pain science and trauma science are converging on a more integrated view.

Stress physiology links emotional history to immune function, pain thresholds, and chronic illness patterns. The chapter foreshadows later discussions of how trauma becomes embodied beyond classic PTSD symptom lists. It also foreshadows controversies about memory, especially recovered memory debates that polarized the 1990s. Van der Kolk presents those controversies as distortions of a deeper truth: memory is not a videotape. Memory is reconstructive and context dependent.

Trauma can disrupt the ordinary stitching of experience into a coherent life story. Flashbulb clarity coexists with perplexing gaps. The chapter explains why simplistic courtroom metaphors about memory failed trauma survivors and science alike. It argues for nuanced methods rather than blanket dismissal of traumatic recall. The neuroscience revolution appears as both promise and hype.

Brain imaging captivated the public imagination with colorful pictures. The chapter cautions that images can seduce people into forgetting complexity. Correlation is not explanation, and snapshots are not lives. Still, the ability to visualize stress-related changes lent legitimacy to invisible suffering. Legitimacy matters when patients have been told their problems are imaginary.

The chapter connects legitimacy to justice, including disability claims and veterans’ benefits. It connects legitimacy to intimate relationships where partners struggle to understand symptoms. Historical psychiatry’s asylum era enters briefly as a warning about coercion and dehumanization. Large institutions sometimes prioritized control over healing. Trauma survivors could be confined and punished for behaviors rooted in survival.

The chapter implies that humane care requires structures that resist repeating trauma’s dynamics. It implies that power asymmetries in treatment settings must be actively managed. The rise of psychopharmacology changed the landscape again. Medications can reduce suffering and can also shift attention away from life context. Van der Kolk does not frame medication as inherently wrong.

He frames it as one tool whose overuse can obscure trauma’s role. The chapter asks readers to hold multiple causes simultaneously. Genetics, development, culture, and chance interact with traumatic events. Reductionism in any direction distorts care. The chapter’s narrative returns to the clinic as the place where abstractions become personal.

A patient’s story can contradict textbook orderliness. Symptoms may cluster oddly. Trauma histories may emerge slowly as trust grows. The chapter validates careful pacing and attunement as ethically necessary, not merely stylistic preferences. It validates curiosity about what happened to a person, not only what is wrong inside them.

The history of mind–brain dualism casts a long shadow over trauma. Descartes’s legacy is not named on every page, yet its habits persist in splitting body from mind in medical training. The chapter advocates integration as a scientific and clinical stance. Integration means linking physiology with meaning without collapsing one into the other. It means recognizing that emotions have substrates without reducing emotions to chemicals alone.

Van der Kolk describes how trauma can interfere with the brain’s capacity to narrate experience in words. Language centers and emotional circuits can become poorly coordinated under chronic stress. That incoordination helps explain why talk therapy alone sometimes stalls. The chapter sets up later body-based approaches as responses to a real clinical problem, not as anti-intellectual trends. Cultural narratives about toughness influence who receives compassion.

Soldiers were expected to endure; women were expected to be delicate; neither stereotype fits trauma’s reality. The chapter critiques stereotypes without denying patterned social harm. It highlights how poverty and discrimination increase exposure to trauma and decrease access to care. Public health framing emerges as inseparable from clinical psychiatry. The chapter positions trauma as a societal issue carried into private suffering.

Science’s revolutions are not only ideas but institutions: universities, journals, funding agencies. Research priorities determine what becomes known quickly and what languishes. Van der Kolk implies that trauma research historically competed with other psychiatric agendas. That competition slowed recognition of developmental trauma for years. The chapter encourages readers to notice what questions researchers could not yet ask because tools and concepts were missing.

It encourages humility about present certainty. Measurement revolutions matter: standardized interviews, structured scales, longitudinal studies. They improved communication among clinicians and researchers. They also risked flattening experience into numbers if used mechanically. The chapter advocates disciplined measurement without worshipping measurement.

Clinical intuition and quantitative rigor are portrayed as partners needing balance. The history of dissociation includes phenomena like fugue states, amnesia, and identity fragmentation. These phenomena challenge simple models of a unified self. Trauma survivors sometimes describe feeling not fully present or not fully continuous over time. The chapter validates those experiences as psychologically intelligible within stress models.

It warns against interpreting dissociation as mere theatricality. Suggestion and fantasy exist, but so does real fragmentation under overwhelm. The chapter discusses how trauma interacts with attachment and development, previewing later chapters on children. Early environments shape the stress system’s calibration. Later traumatic events land on an already shaped nervous system.

The interaction between history and event complicates single-factor explanations. Van der Kolk emphasizes developmental timing without deterministic fatalism. Brains retain plasticity; healing remains possible though often difficult. The chapter’s historical sweep includes shifting attitudes toward emotion itself. Some eras prized stoicism and treated feeling as weakness.

Other eras prized disclosure and risked retraumatization through forced confession. Trauma treatment requires discernment about when to encourage telling and when to stabilize first. The chapter foreshadows phased models without detailing them yet. It also addresses the mind in society: crowds, propaganda, collective violence. Trauma is not only individual; it can be politically manufactured.

Understanding mind and brain includes understanding institutions that organize fear and belonging. The chapter returns to neuroscience as a language that can bridge disciplines if used carefully. It can help therapists, educators, and policymakers speak a shared partial vocabulary. It can also mislead if turned into a new dogma. Van der Kolk encourages readers to treat brain science as a map, not the territory.

He encourages linking maps to patients’ lived environments. The chapter critiques the fantasy of a perfect pill or scan that ends trauma’s complexity. It affirms nonetheless that incremental knowledge reduces gratuitous suffering. Historical case material illustrates how patients’ bodies spoke when words failed. Tremors, paralysis-like states, and sensory alterations appeared in clinical descriptions long before modern imaging.

Clinicians debated whether such signs were neurological or psychological without a clean divide. Trauma often exposes the inadequacy of that divide. The chapter suggests that psychosomatic integration is not a special case but a basic truth of human life. Stress writes into skin, gut, heart, and immune function. The mind–brain revolution includes psychoneuroimmunology and the study of chronic stress cascades.

These fields support a holistic view aligned with trauma survivors’ descriptions. The chapter prepares readers to reject both mind–body dualism and mystical vagueness. It seeks a middle path: precise, embodied, and humane. Philosophy of science appears lightly: paradigms shift when anomalies accumulate. Trauma survivors were often the anomalies that cracked older frameworks.

Their symptoms did not fit tidy categories of sin, weakness, or purely physical disease. Reconceptualization followed pressure from reality, not only from genius. The chapter honors patients as teachers of theory, not only objects of it. Ethical themes thread through scientific change. Research ethics in trauma must contend with vulnerability and suggestibility.

Therapeutic ethics must contend with power and dependency. The chapter warns against charismatic certainty in clinicians and gurus alike. It advocates informed consent and collaboration. The gender politics of psychiatry appear in how hysteria was gendered and how combat trauma was masculinized. Both genderings distorted who was believed.
