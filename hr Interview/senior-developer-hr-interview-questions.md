### 1.1 ðŸ”· **Tell me about a time when you mentored junior developers. What was your approach?**

**Sample Answer:**
"In my previous role, I mentored two junior developers over a six-month period. My approach focused on three key areas:

First, I established regular one-on-one sessions where we reviewed their code together, discussing not just what to improve but why certain patterns are preferred. I used the Socratic method, asking questions that led them to discover solutions themselves rather than simply providing answers.

Second, I paired with them on complex features, gradually transitioning from me driving while they observed, to them driving while I guided, and finally to them working independently with periodic check-ins.

Third, I created a safe environment for mistakes by emphasizing that errors are learning opportunities. When they encountered bugs in production, we'd walk through the debugging process together and discuss how to prevent similar issues in the future.

The result was that both developers became confident contributors who could handle medium-complexity tasks independently within four months, and one of them is now mentoring others."

**Sample Answer:**

### 1.4 ðŸ”· **How do you motivate team members who are underperforming?**

**Sample Answer:**
"When I notice underperformance, I first seek to understand the root cause rather than jumping to conclusions. I schedule a private one-on-one conversation to understand what's happening.

- Unclear expectations or goals
- Personal challenges affecting work
- Lack of necessary skills or resources
- Misalignment between the work and their interests
- Burnout or overwhelming workload

Once I understand the cause, I work collaboratively to create an improvement plan. For example, I had a developer whose performance dropped significantly. Through conversation, I learned they were dealing with personal issues and felt overwhelmed by the project complexity.

We agreed on a plan: temporarily reducing their workload, pairing them with a senior developer for complex tasks, and having weekly check-ins to monitor progress and provide support. I also connected them with company resources for personal support.

Within two months, their performance improved significantly. They appreciated that I approached the situation with empathy rather than punishment, which strengthened our working relationship and their commitment to the team."

### 1.8 ðŸ”· **How do you handle disagreements about technical decisions with other senior team members?**

**Sample Answer:**
"Disagreements among senior developers are natural and can lead to better solutions if handled constructively. My approach:

**1. Listen actively:**
I ensure I fully understand the other person's perspective, asking clarifying questions and restating their position to confirm understanding.

**2. Focus on data and objectives:**
I shift the conversation from opinions to measurable criteriaâ€”performance benchmarks, maintainability, team expertise, time constraints, and business requirements.

**3. Find common ground:**
Usually, we agree on the goals even if we disagree on the approach. I start there and work forward.

**4. Suggest proof-of-concepts:**
When the debate is theoretical, I propose building small prototypes of competing approaches to evaluate them objectively.

**5. Know when to escalate or compromise:**
If we can't reach consensus and the decision is critical, I involve relevant stakeholders or suggest a time-boxed trial of one approach with clear success metrics.

**Example:**
I once disagreed with another senior developer about database choice for a new serviceâ€”I advocated for PostgreSQL while they preferred MongoDB. Instead of continuing the debate, we defined our criteria: query patterns, scaling needs, team expertise, and operational complexity.

We built small prototypes with realistic data and load tests. The data showed PostgreSQL better met our specific use case, and my colleague agreed. Later, for a different service with different requirements, we chose MongoDB based on the same criteria-driven approach.

The key is maintaining mutual respect and recognizing that reasonable people can disagree while keeping the team's best interests in mind."
